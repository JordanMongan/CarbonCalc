Use public transport

Using public transport is one of the most effective and accessible ways in which to reduce your overall transportation carbon footprint. Data shows that, on average, individuals who use public transport produce 2800kg less carbon than those that rely on a car. Oh, and it’ll save you some money, too!

Pedal power!

Riding your bike is an affordable, efficient way to travel, with cyclists being able to use designated lanes to zoom past traffic jams. It's also a fantastic way to do more physical activity and have fun in the process. Encourage your teams by promoting the Cycle To Work Scheme

Walk short journeys

Where possible, consider walking shorter journeys. It’s a great way to get more active without having to set foot in (or pay for) a gym and is proven to work wonders for our mental health, energy levels, and sleep routines!

Switch to public transportation

Public transportation provides a fantastic way for us to reduce our carbon footprints, being easily accessible, cost-effective, and often quicker than driving. Data shows that, on average, individuals who use public transport produce 2800kg less carbon per year than those that rely on a car.

Carpooling

Travelling in groups instead of driving solo reduces the number of cars on the roads. This reduces the carbon footprints of the individual passengers, helps to minimise congestion during rush hours, and means that we’re spending less on fuel! Who knows, there might even be a bit of karaoke too?

Accelerate and break smoothly

When driving, try to keep accelerating and braking to a minimum. By speeding away only to slam on the brakes, we can burn up to 30% more fuel and shorten the lifespans of our vehicles. Try using features such as cruise control to keep your speed constant.

Research alternaitve transport

Air travel has the worst carbon footprint of all modes of transport. For shorter journeys such as domestic flights between Edinburgh and London, try researching if it's possible to travel by train instead as this can reduce your emissions by close to 85%

Pack light

The more you try to squeeze into your suitcase, the heavier it will be. Heavy bags means heavy aircrafts which will need more fuel to stay in the air. Consider streamlining your packing to just the essentials or limiting yourself to whatever you can fit in a carry-on case!

Opt for economy

Flying business class might be more comfortable, but these seats don’t maximise a plane’s passenger capacity. As well as being cheaper, choosing to sit in economy means airlines can fit more people on a plane and reduce the number of flights they have to schedule to meet demand.

Choo-choo-choose the train

Choo-choo-choose the train! Data shows that travelling by train produces significantly less CO2e than flying or driving, even in the case of electric vehicles with multiple passengers. What’s more, planes produce a greater number of greenhouse gases which can further accelerate the rate of global warming.

Offset emissions

Even though trains produce significantly less CO2e than other modes of transport, the rail industry as a whole does still contribute to overall global emissions. Learn how you can offset these emissions and even become carbon negative through sites like Ecologi.

Understand carbon intensity

Many people don’t realise that it’s actually better to use electricity at specific times, as the carbon intensity - the amount of CO2 emitted through the production of one kilowatt hour of energy - of this electricity fluctuates throughout the day. Learn more here

User renewable energy sources

Around the world, energy providers are now working to meet growing demands for greener tariffs. By switching to a provider of solar, wind, or hydroelectric energy, you can reduce the carbon footprint of your energy consumption and even save money too!

Buy energy-efficient appliances

Electrical appliances come with an energy rating from A-G to indicate their efficiency based on their product class. Knowing that ‘A’ is the most efficient, we can make more informed decisions about the environmental (and financial) effects of our purchases. Learn more on the Energy Saving Trust’s website.

Keep the cold out

Cold air can seep in from outside and reduce a building’s ambient temperature. Rather than having to compensate for this with heaters, make sure that your business premises are properly insulated by sealing gaps around windows, installing draught-proof wall cavities, and ensuring that doors aren’t left ajar.

Install ceiling insulation

Up to 40% of a building’s heat is lost through roofing that doesn’t have proper insulation. By insulating the ceiling of your office space, you can improve its energy efficiency and make it 5% warmer during the winter. Learn more here

Use a smart meter

Smart meters are a fantastic way to help your business reduce its energy consumption, save time, and manage costs. As well as giving you insights into your usage habits, smart meters automatically send readings to energy suppliers to make sure your business isn’t overpaying. Read more here

Identify and fix leaks

A leaking tap, toilet, or shower head can lose up to 20 gallons of water every day. Use the water consumption data provided by your smart meter to set benchmarks for how much your business is using so you can identify and repair any leaks promptly! Learn more here

Use mulch in landscaping

Keeping gardens or grassy areas green uses a lot of water, especially during the summer months. By covering the ground in a layer of mulch - a mix of decaying leaves and bark - you can prevent water loss through evaporation and keep soil moist for longer. Read more here

Harvest rainwater

Currently, not a lot of urban buildings are designed to collect and store rainwater, but there are simple modifications that can change this. Survey your office building to find out whether it’s possible to harvest rainwater and make the Mains to Rains pledge

Choose green appliances

This doesn't mean buying an emerald fridge or a lime green kettle, it means choosing electrical appliances based on their energy efficiency. Study the energy ratings of different models to make informed decisions about what will use (and cost) the least!

Be a second-hand superstar

Sure, we all like having the latest versions of things, but brand new appliances have significantly higher carbon footprints than refurbished or second-hand equivalents. Choosing to buy pre-owned phones, laptops, and tablets for your employees can reduce your footprint and save you money too!

Use renewable energy

There’s no getting around the fact that businesses need electrical appliances to operate effectively. Minimise the environmental impact of these devices by switching your business onto an energy plan that comes from renewable sources such as solar, wind, and hydroelectric.

Carbon concious booking

By using booking systems such as Staze , we can compare the different hotels in our travel destination according to their carbon footprints. This enables us to make informed decisions about the environmental impact of our business travel and limit it where possible.

Downsize your room

Hotel rooms will have different carbon footprints based on their size, level of luxury, and amenities. An economy room, for example, will produce significantly less CO2e than a deluxe suite, as it will have fewer hard and soft furnishings and require less energy to heat and cool.

Skip breakfast

You don’t have to skip it altogether; it is the most important meal of the day after all! The issue with hotel breakfasts lies in the buffet offering, as nearly 40% of the food provided ends up being binned. Consider breakfasting elsewhere to reduce the carbon footprint of your stay.

Source recycled materials

Wherever possible, look to source recycled raw materials as these have considerably smaller carbon footprints than those obtained through mining. In construction, for example, seek out recycled steel or aluminium and establish partnerships with suppliers who embrace environmentally friendly practices. Learn more here

Buy local

The further that a material is shipped in lorries and planes, the bigger its carbon footprint will be. We can prevent these carbon emissions by sourcing materials locally through platforms such as Enviromate - an online marketplace that sells leftover building materials that would otherwise go to waste.

Streamline material usage

Reducing our overall material usage needs to be factored into a product’s development as early as possible. By designing things to be ‘lighter’ from the outset, we can reduce the carbon emissions generated through manufacturing processes and transportation.

Work from home during summer months

During the summer months, employees tend to use less gas and electricity than in the winter, therefore working from home can reduce your bills along with reducing the amount you commute to the office. Unfortunately, because of the rigidity of conventional working hours, commuters are often stuck in traffic jams accounting for millions of tons of wasted greenhouse gases. Read more here

Work at the office during winter months

During the winter months, households see their utility bills increase significantly, whereas working in the office means a heating carbon footprint that is split over many employees. Moreover, the efficiency of systems in offices are usually higher than at home.

Measure your work electricity and gas usage

Did you know you could charge your employer with some electricity and gas usage at home? If you work from home, you can claim some of your home utility costs as a business expense, saving yourself a bit of tax. What you can claim, and how, depends on whether you’re a company director or sole trader. Read more here

Go virtual

Recent studieshave shown how switching to virtual and hybrid meetings can reduce their carbon footprint by 94% and 67% respectively. This is made possible because less energy is used to heat and illuminate meeting rooms and fewer people are commuting to a central location.

Turn your camera off

Video conferences use significantly more energy than voice calls because there is more data to store and transfer via the internet. Reduce the carbon footprint of your meetings by up to 96% by disabling the cameras on your computers, televisions, tablets, and phones during calls.

Avoid food wastage

Organising meetings at breakfast or lunch time means providing things like pastries and sandwiches for attendees. Much of this ends up being thrown away which is a waste of the food, its carbon footprint, and of your money. Avoid this by ordering less or scheduling meetings at other times.

Send fewer emails

Yes, even emails have carbon footprints. Analysts have calculated that, on average, a year of emailing emits close to 140kg - the same as driving 200 miles in a petrol car! Limit this by thinking twice before sending emails and keeping any attachments as small as possible.

Empty your inboxes

Storing emails in the data centres of cloud-based platforms requires huge amounts of energy. Reduce the carbon footprint of your email inboxes by deleting any unnecessary messages (especially those with large attachments) and emptying your junk folders regularly. Learn more here

Unsubscribing

According to The Good Planet, nearly 110 billion spam emails are received every day, emitting the same CO2e as 3.1 million petrol-powered cars! One way to reduce the number of emails being sent and stored is to unsubscribe from marketing newsletters that you don’t intend to read.

Print double-sided

This might be self-evident, but double-sided printing is an effective, accessible way to reduce the amount of paper we waste by up to 40%. Printers can be configured to print on both sides automatically in their settings, learn how here

Using recycled printing paper

The process of recycling paper generates 20% less CO2e than is produced during incineration. Moreover, manufacturing one tonne of virgin paper fibres requires 35,000 more litres of water than is needed to produce the same amount of recycled paper. Use paper from recycled sources where possible.

Out with the old

Older printers are more than capable of meeting the small-scale requirements of most offices, but become inefficient when asked to work at higher capacities. Save on power by investing in a newer, more energy efficient machine that’s designed for large volume printing. Bonus points for buying one second-hand!

Adjust power settings

Devices like computers, laptops, and televisions have settings that cause them to go into sleep or hibernation modes automatically after periods of inactivity. This allows you to prevent them from using energy unnecessarily when not in use and preserve the battery life of portable devices!

Reduce your screen brightness

Brighter screens consume more energy when in use. Fortunately, studies show that by dimming your monitor’s brightness from 100% to 70%, you can instantly reduce its energy consumption by a fifth. Oh, and it’ll give your eyes a much-needed rest, too!

Explore offsetting initiatives

Ecosia - a free browser extension that funds tree planting schemes from its profits - enables you to offset the CO2e you produce by using electronic devices. Encourage your employees to add Ecosia onto their search engines to support its projects and offset your business’s emissions.

Explore carbon offsetting

Carbon offsetting has become something of a swear word in climate circles. There’s no end of very credible arguments for this – perhaps, most importantly, that ultimately offsetting does little to encourage actual behavioural change, which is what’s required if we’re to mitigate the worst parts of climate change. However, if there’s one thing we can be certain of, it’s that society is slow to change.

Manage assets

Much of digital ad serving still relies on downloading content to the end user’s device. This means one of the largest contributors to CO2 emissions within the digital media space is the serving of the creative itself.

Don't waste time

As a general rule, the busier an energy grid is, the less efficient (and more carbon-intensive) it becomes. So running just 5% of campaigns during non-peak times can have a significant impact on the amount of CO2 emissions they generate.

How to use this carbon footprint calculator

This carbon calculator tool has been developed by Hattrick to help businesses like yours keep track of the CO2e (carbon dioxide equivalent) emissions they produce. You will already be familiar with some of the categories, but there may well be some that come as a surprise. The hope is that, by empowering more people with the knowledge and insights about the environmental impacts of their business operations, we can work together to slow, stop, and reverse the potentially catastrophic effects of climate change.

On the first tab of this calculator, you will input information about your day-to-day business activities that generate carbon emissions. It may be helpful to gather all of this information before you start. When you have finished filling in all of a category’s fields, the relevant button will change from blue to grey.

The second tab will provide you with recommendations for how you can reduce your carbon emissions, based on your current footprint. These are colour-coded using a traffic light system to indicate the environmental impacts of your current operating practices.

Audit your website

By using tools such as Wholegrain Digital’s Website Carbon Calculator, businesses can identify the aspects of their websites that generate the most CO2e emissions. The insights provided can then be used to make updates, changes, and improvements where necessary.

Content & SEO

Search engines analyse the quality of web content to make it as quick and easy as possible for users to find relevant, valuable information. Better quality content means people will spend less time browsing which, in turn, reduces the carbon footprint of your website. Learn more here

Use fewer images and videos

Photos and videos have the largest file sizes and are the biggest contributor to a webpage’s weight. By choosing to use smaller, lower resolution images (or, where they don’t add value for the user, removing them entirely) businesses can reduce the amount of energy required to browse their website.

Choose your channels carefully

Different social media channels have different carbon footprints based on the amount of energy it takes to load and interact with content in your feed. Calculate the CO2e emissions generated through your business’s social media activities using Compare The Market’s online tool.

Set usage limits

Limiting ourselves to a set amount of time on social media each day is one of the easiest ways to reduce the carbon emissions generated by stopping us from mindlessly scrolling our newsfeeds. Install browser extensions to block social media sites and disable notifications on your devices outside certain times.

Post and share less

Each action we perform on social media contributes to our overall carbon footprint. Encourage your team to be more strategic with the content it posts and shares to generate the same results with less activity. Providing best practice training is an effective way to do this.

Opt for online

Conference venues and exhibition halls consume a lot of energy in heating and lighting, whilst the buildings themselves have large quantities of embodied carbon. Studies show that moving events online can reduce their carbon footprints and energy usages by 94% and 90% respectively.

Hybrid hubs

A lot of the CO2e emissions generated by conferences, events, and exhibitions comes from attendees travelling from all over the world to a single location. Overcome this by attending events that have hubs in various different locations to minimise the distances your teams have to travel.

Provide sustainable food

If in-person events are non-negotiable, consider providing vegan or vegetarian food and drink for delegates as the carbon footprints of things like meat and dairy are dramatically bigger than plant-based alternatives. Learn more here

Keep inboxes empty

Many of us are guilty of holding onto emails, SMS messages, and social media chats for longer than necessary. The storage of these requires huge amounts of space in cloud-based servers that have large carbon footprints. Reduce this by regularly cleansing your inboxes and chat histories.

Emails

Whether it’s for marketing or managing client relations, every email we send generates greenhouse gas emissions. Limit this as much as possible by only sending essential emails, both internally and externally, and avoid copying in lots of users unnecessarily. Social media

The majority of businesses have a presence on social media, be it on one or a number of platforms. All social media activity has a carbon footprint, so limiting this as much as possible is an effective way to reduce the CO2e generated. Learn more from the social media recommendations.

Campaign calculator

Use tools such as Good-Loop’s carbon calculator to work out how much CO2e your business’s advertising campaigns are generating. Then, using the insights provided, optimise your production and distribution processes to reduce their carbon footprint where possible.

Sustainable shoots

Producing video content generates a lot of carbon emissions, be it through travelling to filming destinations or building sets. Limit these emissions by repurposing existing content where possible and, if new video collateral is required, use filming locations that are nearby. Learn more here.

Use targeted advertising

Think about the audience demographic that you are trying to reach with your ad campaign and optimise it accordingly. Targeting in this way will reduce the number of pointless impressions (all of which have a carbon footprint) and maximise the potential of your PPC budget!
